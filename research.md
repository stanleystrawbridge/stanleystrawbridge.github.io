# **Research**

---

<br>

## Plain language

---

A human embryo begins as a single cell when a sperm fertilises an egg.
Over the next seven days, the number of cells increases through repeated divisions.
During this time, the new cells begin to decide what type of cell they will become, such as those that form the baby or those that support its growth.
Nearly three-quarters of pregnancy losses happen shortly after this stage, when the embryo tries to implant into the lining of the uterus, called the endometrium.

We study how these choices are made and what happens when they go wrong.
Using specialised microscopes, we observe how cells in human embryos and in embryo models made from stem cells arrange themselves and change over time.
We then use computer models to test and refine our ideas.

Our goal is to uncover the rules of early human development so we can better understand fertility, miscarriage, and the origins of certain diseases.

## Technical overview

---

The Strawbridge Lab studies how early mammalian embryos organise themselves in space and time.
We focus on the period from fertilisation to implantation, when initially similar cells begin to acquire distinct identities and arrange into the first embryonic and extra-embryonic tissues.
This is a critical developmental window, but it remains difficult to study directly in humans because implantation occurs soon after the embryo enters the uterus.

Our research combines human embryo biology, stem cell models, quantitative imaging, image analysis, and mathematical modelling.
We use human pluripotent stem cells, embryo-like models, mouse embryos, and engineered implantation-relevant systems to ask how cell fate, cell position, tissue geometry, and signalling are coordinated during early development.

### Cell-state transitions and developmental timing

A major focus of the lab is understanding how cells move between developmental states.
We study how pluripotent cells exit self-renewal, acquire new identities, and respond to differentiation signals.
Rather than treating cell fate as a simple endpoint, we analyse the timing, variability, and probability of cell-state transitions across populations.

This allows us to ask whether developmental transitions occur through uniform, asynchronous, delayed, or heterogeneous processes.
We use quantitative models to describe these dynamics and to identify parameters that can be compared across experiments, perturbations, and biological systems.

### Spatial signalling and lineage patterning

Early embryos do not only make the right cell types.
They must also place them in the right positions.
We study how signalling, cell-cell interactions, tissue geometry, and mechanical constraints influence lineage patterning during preimplantation development.

A particular interest is how local signalling environments are shaped by the spatial organisation of cells.
By combining experimental perturbations with computational analysis, we aim to understand how embryos generate robust patterning despite variability in cell number, position, and developmental timing.

### Human embryo models and implantation

Implantation is one of the least accessible stages of human development, yet it is also one of the stages at which pregnancy loss is most common.
We are developing stem-cell-based and bioengineered systems to model interactions between embryo-like structures and endometrial tissues in vitro.

These systems allow us to study how early human tissues organise, attach, signal, and respond to their environment.
They also provide experimentally tractable platforms for investigating mechanisms that may contribute to implantation failure, miscarriage, and early developmental disorders.

### Quantitative imaging and image analysis

We use advanced microscopy to measure developmental processes directly.
This includes live imaging, three-dimensional imaging, segmentation, cell tracking, lineage classification, and quantitative analysis of tissue architecture.

Our aim is to move beyond descriptive imaging towards measurements that can be used to test specific biological hypotheses.
We therefore develop and apply computational tools to extract information about cell position, cell shape, marker expression, tissue organisation, and dynamic behaviour from complex image datasets.

### Mathematical and computational modelling

Mathematical modelling is central to our approach.
We use models to formalise biological ideas, identify which mechanisms are consistent with the data, and guide future experiments.
These models range from statistical descriptions of cell-state transition kinetics to spatial models of signalling, tissue organisation, and cell-cell interaction.

The goal is not to replace experiments, but to make experimental reasoning more precise.
By linking model parameters to measurable biological quantities, we aim to build a predictive understanding of early development.

### Experimental to theoretical workflow

Our work follows an iterative workflow.
We first define a biological question and identify the relevant experimental system.
We then measure developmental dynamics using imaging, molecular assays, or quantitative phenotyping.
Computational analysis is used to extract interpretable features from the data, and mathematical models are used to test candidate mechanisms.

Predictions from these models are then used to design new experiments.
In this way, experiments and theory are developed together, with each informing the other.

### Long-term goal

The long-term goal of the lab is to develop a quantitative and predictive understanding of early human development.
By uncovering how cells make decisions, communicate, and organise into tissues, we aim to better understand the biological basis of fertility, miscarriage, and developmental disease.
